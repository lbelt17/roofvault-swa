26. What types of construction joints can be used for a water stop? Choose Two.

A. Construction.

B. Recessed.

C. Expansion.

D. Contraction.

     

        Why are other options incorrect:

❌ B. Recessed: This term generally refers to a decorative or functional architectural detail  

           rather than a structural joint type designed for water stop integration.

❌ D. Contraction: While contraction joints (also known as control joints) control where

concrete cracks due to shrinkage, they are most frequently sealed with surface-applied sealants rather than embedded water stops. While specific water-stop profiles can be used in contraction joints where heavy movement is expected, they are not the primary application for construction and expansion joints.

84. To calculate the hydrostatic pressure at 30 feet below grade against a wall:  formula: 

       Pressure = (Density of water) x (Gravity) x (Depth). Assuming the density of water is 62.4 lbs./ft³ and

       Gravity is 32.2 ft/s²; the pressure at 30 feet below grade would be approximately 19,992 Pascals (Pa)

       

Calculation breakdown: 

  • Pressure = (62.4 lbs./ft³) x (32.2 ft/s²) x (30 ft) 
  • Pressure = 59,904 lbs./ft² 
  • Convert to psi: Pressure = 59,904 lbs./ft² / 144 in²/ft² = 416 psi

For hot rubberized asphalt (HRA) waterproofing systems, the standard minimum distance for clearing penetrations and drains with flashing or reinforcing materials is generally 6 inches. 

Installation guidelines typically specify:

  • Reinforcement Extension: Reinforcing fabric or elastomeric sheets must extend to a minimum of 6 inches beyond all deck drains, penetrations, and joints.
  • Flashing Overlap: Neoprene flashing or elastomeric sheets must overlap subsequent membrane connections by at least 6 inches.
  • Seal Integrity: To ensure a proper seal, no splices or seams are permitted within 3 inches of a drain flange or penetration. 

While 6 inches is the standard for membrane detailing, some industry best practices (such as NRCA recommendations) suggest a larger clearance of 12 inches between separate penetrations to ensure installers have enough physical space to properly apply the hot rubber and flashing.

113. Epoxy Injection Methods:

  • Cross-stitching: This pattern is often called "stitch grouting" and ensures that the injection material has a better chance of reaching the entire length of the crack.
  • Intersection: Drilling at a 45-degree angle allows the drill to intersect the crack halfway through the concrete thickness, which is the ideal target for a complete fill.
  • Structural integrity: By alternating sides and using a 45-degree angle, you avoid creating a "weak" side by drilling too many holes on one side, which can be compromised by the injection pressure.
  • Pressure distribution: The staggered pattern distributes the injection pressure more evenly, helping to prevent concrete spalling or cracking during the process. 

How to execute the technique

  1. Drill holes: Drill holes for the injection ports at a 45-degree angle, alternating from one side of the crack to the other. The distance from the crack should be approximately half the thickness of the concrete element (e.g., for a 10-inch-thick wall, drill 5 inches from the crack).
  2. Install packers: Install the injection packers into the drilled holes.
  3. Inject epoxy: Begin injecting at the lowest port first, moving upward along the crack. Once the epoxy starts to flow out of a port further up the crack, stop injecting at the current port and move to the next one
