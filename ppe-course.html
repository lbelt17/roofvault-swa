Lesson Summaries

1

Why PPE Matters on a Roofing Jobsite

PPE is the first layer of protection before tools, materials, or production speed.

Section 1 — What PPE Means

PPE means Personal Protective Equipment. It is the gear worn to help protect your body from jobsite hazards. On a roofing job, PPE may include a hard hat, safety glasses, gloves, proper boots, hearing protection, high-visibility clothing, fall protection, respiratory protection, and weather protection.

Key points

  • PPE helps protect your head, eyes, hands, feet, lungs, hearing, and body.
  • PPE is selected based on the task and the hazards around you.
  • PPE does not remove the hazard; it helps reduce the chance of injury.

Field takeawayPPE is not just gear. It is part of the safety plan.

Section 2 — Why Roofers Need PPE

Roofing work exposes workers to hazards that can change throughout the day. A roof may be dry in the morning and slippery later. A simple material-handling task can turn dangerous if tools, debris, heat, chemicals, or moving equipment are nearby.

Key points

  • Roofers may face falls, sharp edges, hot surfaces, chemicals, dust, noise, falling objects, and bad weather.
  • Different tasks require different PPE.
  • Wearing PPE correctly helps prevent small mistakes from becoming serious injuries.

Field takeawayThe roof environment changes fast. Your protection has to match the work.

Section 3 — PPE Must Be Worn Correctly

PPE only works when it fits, is worn correctly, and is used for the right task. Wearing PPE the wrong way can give a worker false confidence.

Key points

  • Safety glasses should protect the eyes, not sit on top of the head.
  • Gloves should match the hazard, such as cuts, heat, or chemicals.
  • Boots should have proper traction and be in good condition.
  • Respirators must be the right type and fit properly.
  • Damaged or worn-out PPE should be reported and replaced.

Field takeawayPPE that is worn wrong may not protect you when you need it.

Section 4 — PPE Has Limits

PPE is important, but it is not magic. PPE should not be the only safety control. Workers still need safe work practices, hazard awareness, training, and supervision.

Key points

  • PPE does not stop a fall from happening.
  • Gloves do not make sharp metal safe to handle carelessly.
  • Safety glasses do not replace safe cutting or grinding practices.
  • Respirators do not fix poor ventilation or unknown chemical hazards.
  • PPE works best when combined with good safety habits.

Field takeawayPPE helps protect you, but you still have to work safely.

Section 5 — Before Starting Work

Before beginning a roofing task, stop and check the hazard, the task, and the required PPE. If you are unsure what PPE is needed, ask a supervisor before starting.

Key points

  • What task am I doing?
  • What can hurt me?
  • What PPE is required?
  • Is my PPE clean, working, and undamaged?
  • Am I wearing it correctly?
  • Do I need to stop and ask before continuing?

Field takeawayCheck your PPE before the job starts, not after something goes wrong.

Section 6 — Quick Knowledge Check

Before starting a roofing task, what should a worker do first?

  • A. Start working and adjust PPE later
  • B. Check the task, hazards, and required PPE
  • C. Only wear PPE if the job looks dangerous
  • D. Let another worker decide

Correct answer: B — Check the task, hazards, and required PPE.

2

Hard Hats and Head Protection

Head protection helps protect roofers from falling objects, flying debris, bumps, overhead hazards, and certain electrical hazards.

Section 1 — Why Head Protection Matters

On a roofing jobsite, head injuries can happen quickly. Tools, fasteners, materials, buckets, debris, and equipment can fall or move unexpectedly. Roofers may also work around ladders, hoists, cranes, scaffolds, roof edges, low beams, and overhead work.

Key points

  • A hard hat helps protect the head from impact.
  • Head protection may be needed when objects can fall from above.
  • Head protection may be needed when flying debris is possible.
  • Head protection may be needed near overhead work or equipment.
  • Some jobs may require helmets rated for electrical hazards.

Field takeawayIf something can hit your head, fall toward you, or fly toward you, head protection matters.

Section 2 — When Roofers Should Wear Head Protection

Roofers should wear head protection whenever the jobsite has a possible head-injury hazard. The need depends on the work being done, the people working nearby, and the hazards around the roof.

Key points

  • Wear head protection when workers are above you.
  • Wear head protection when tools or materials are being lifted.
  • Wear head protection around cranes, forklifts, hoists, or loading areas.
  • Wear head protection near demolition, tear-off, or debris removal.
  • Wear head protection when the company, site, or supervisor requires it.

Field takeawayDo not wait until something falls. Put head protection on before entering active work areas.

Section 3 — Hard Hat Fit and Proper Use

A hard hat must fit correctly and be worn the way it was designed. If it is loose, backwards without approval, tilted, cracked, or modified, it may not protect the worker properly.

Key points

  • Adjust the suspension so the hard hat fits securely.
  • Keep the hard hat level on the head.
  • Do not wear it loose or barely sitting on the head.
  • Do not drill holes, cut, paint, or modify it unless allowed by the manufacturer.
  • Do not wear it backwards unless the hard hat is designed or approved for that use.

Field takeawayA hard hat only helps when it is worn correctly.

Section 4 — Inspecting a Hard Hat

Hard hats should be checked before use. Heat, sunlight, impact, chemicals, age, and rough handling can weaken the shell or suspension system.

Key points

  • Look for cracks, dents, holes, chalky surfaces, or brittleness.
  • Check the suspension straps for damage or wear.
  • Replace the hard hat after a strong impact, even if it looks okay.
  • Remove damaged hard hats from service.
  • Follow the manufacturer’s replacement guidance.

Field takeawayIf the hard hat is damaged, do not trust it.

Section 5 — Common Roofing Head Hazards

Roofing work creates many head hazards because people, tools, and materials often move at the same time. A worker may be safe one minute and exposed the next.

Key points

  • Falling tools, screws, plates, fasteners, rolls, buckets, and debris.
  • Flying debris from cutting, grinding, tear-off, or wind.
  • Low beams, roof hatches, ladders, equipment, and pipe supports.
  • Material loading zones and hoisting areas.
  • Electrical hazards near exposed conductors or energized equipment.

Field takeawayLook up, look around, and think about what could move or fall.

Section 6 — What a Hard Hat Does Not Do

A hard hat is important, but it does not make unsafe work safe. Workers still need safe material handling, controlled loading zones, proper communication, and awareness of overhead hazards.

Key points

  • A hard hat does not stop objects from falling.
  • A hard hat does not replace good housekeeping.
  • A hard hat does not protect the face or eyes by itself.
  • A hard hat does not protect against every electrical hazard.
  • A hard hat does not remove the need to stay out of drop zones.

Field takeawayHead protection helps reduce injury, but the safer move is preventing the strike in the first place.

Section 7 — Quick Knowledge Check

When should a roofer wear head protection?

  • A. Only when the roof is steep
  • B. Only during hot work
  • C. When there is a possible danger from impact, falling or flying objects, overhead hazards, or required site rules
  • D. Only after materials are already being lifted

Correct answer: C — Wear head protection when there is a possible head-injury hazard or when the jobsite requires it.

3

Eye and Face Protection

Eye and face protection helps protect roofers from dust, debris, chemicals, blades, fasteners, and flying particles.

Section 1 — Common Eye and Face Hazards

Roofing work can create hazards that hit the eyes or face without warning. Small particles, dust, liquid splash, sparks, and flying debris can cause serious injuries.

Key points

  • Dust and debris from tear-off, sweeping, cutting, and grinding.
  • Flying fasteners, metal pieces, granules, chips, and particles.
  • Chemical splash from coatings, cleaners, primers, adhesives, or solvents.
  • Bright light, sparks, or hot particles from certain tools or hot work.
  • Wind can blow dust or loose material into the face.

Field takeawayIf debris, dust, liquid, sparks, or particles can reach your face, protect your eyes and face first.

Section 2 — Choosing the Right Protection

Different tasks may require different protection. Safety glasses may be enough for some work, while goggles or a face shield may be needed for higher-risk tasks.

Key points

  • Safety glasses help protect against basic flying particles.
  • Goggles may be needed when dust or splash can come from different directions.
  • Face shields help protect the face, but they are usually worn with safety glasses or goggles.
  • Chemical work may require splash-rated eye protection.
  • Follow the jobsite, supervisor, or product safety instructions.

Field takeawayMatch the eye and face protection to the task, not just the job title.

Section 3 — Proper Use and Inspection

Eye and face protection only works when it is clean, fits correctly, and is worn during the task. Damaged or dirty lenses can reduce visibility and safety.

Key points

  • Wear eye protection over the eyes, not on top of the head.
  • Clean dirty lenses before the task starts.
  • Replace scratched, cracked, or broken lenses.
  • Make sure goggles or shields fit securely.
  • Do not remove eye protection while hazards are still present.

Field takeawayEye protection cannot help if it is dirty, damaged, or not being worn.

Quick Knowledge Check

When should a roofer use eye or face protection?

  • A. Only when someone is watching
  • B. Only after debris starts flying
  • C. When dust, flying particles, chemicals, sparks, or splash hazards are possible
  • D. Only when working on a steep roof

Correct answer: C — Use eye or face protection before the hazard reaches your eyes or face.

Gloves and Hand Protection

Hand protection helps roofers reduce injuries from sharp edges, hot materials, chemicals, adhesives, tools, and rough surfaces.

Section 1 — Common Hand Hazards

Roofers use their hands constantly. Cuts, burns, punctures, chemical contact, and crushed fingers can happen during normal work if the wrong gloves are used or no gloves are worn.

Key points

  • Sharp metal, flashing, fasteners, knives, blades, and torn material.
  • Hot materials, hot surfaces, torches, or heated equipment.
  • Chemicals, adhesives, coatings, cleaners, primers, and solvents.
  • Rough surfaces, splinters, pinch points, and moving materials.
  • Wet or slippery materials that are hard to grip.

Field takeawayYour hands are always exposed, so glove choice matters.

Section 2 — Choosing the Right Glove

No single glove is right for every roofing task. A glove that works for material handling may not protect against chemicals or heat.

Key points

  • Cut-resistant gloves may be needed for sharp metal or blades.
  • Chemical-resistant gloves may be needed for coatings, adhesives, primers, or solvents.
  • Heat-resistant gloves may be needed around hot materials or hot work.
  • Grip gloves may help with material handling.
  • The product label, SDS, supervisor, or company rules may identify required gloves.

Field takeawayMatch the glove to the hazard.

Section 3 — Proper Use and Inspection

Gloves should fit correctly and be checked before use. Loose, torn, soaked, contaminated, or worn-out gloves may create more risk instead of reducing it.

Key points

  • Check for holes, tears, stiffness, contamination, or worn palms.
  • Do not use chemical-soaked gloves without direction.
  • Remove gloves safely if chemicals are on them.
  • Do not wear loose gloves near moving parts where they can catch.
  • Replace damaged gloves before continuing work.

Field takeawayA damaged glove can give false confidence.

Quick Knowledge Check

What is the best way to choose gloves for a roofing task?

  • A. Use the same gloves for every job
  • B. Pick the cheapest gloves available
  • C. Match the glove to the hazard, such as cuts, heat, or chemicals
  • D. Only wear gloves when carrying heavy rolls

Correct answer: C — Gloves should match the hazard.

5

Footwear and Slip Protection

Proper roofing footwear helps with traction, stability, puncture protection, and safer movement on roof surfaces.

Section 1 — Common Foot and Slip Hazards

Roof surfaces can become slippery or unstable because of weather, materials, dust, coatings, granules, oil, debris, or slope. Poor footwear increases the chance of slips and foot injuries.

Key points

  • Wet roofs, coatings, dust, loose granules, and debris.
  • Steep slopes, ladders, roof edges, and uneven surfaces.
  • Nails, screws, plates, sharp objects, and rough materials.
  • Heavy rolls, buckets, tools, or materials dropped near the feet.
  • Worn-out soles that no longer grip the surface.

Field takeawayBad footing can turn a simple step into a serious incident.

Section 2 — Choosing Proper Footwear

Roofing footwear should fit the jobsite and the work being done. Boots or shoes should provide traction, support, and protection based on the hazards present.

Key points

  • Use footwear with good traction for the roof surface.
  • Use puncture-resistant or safety-toe footwear when required.
  • Avoid worn-out soles, slick bottoms, or damaged boots.
  • Keep laces tied and secure.
  • Follow company, site, or manufacturer requirements.

Field takeawayYour boots are part of your fall prevention.

Section 3 — Proper Use and Inspection

Footwear should be checked before stepping onto the roof. Even good boots can become unsafe when the soles are worn, contaminated, or damaged.

Key points

  • Inspect soles for wear, cuts, separation, or slick spots.
  • Remove mud, oil, wet coating, or debris from footwear when possible.
  • Do not use damaged footwear on active roof work.
  • Pay attention when moving from ladder to roof surface.
  • Slow down when the roof condition changes.

Field takeawayCheck your footing before trusting your next step.

Quick Knowledge Check

Why does footwear matter on a roofing jobsite?

  • A. It only matters for appearance
  • B. It helps with traction, stability, and protection from foot hazards
  • C. It replaces fall protection
  • D. It only matters in cold weather

Correct answer: B — Proper footwear helps with traction, stability, and foot protection.

Hearing Protection

Hearing protection helps protect roofers from loud tools and equipment that can damage hearing over time.

Section 1 — Common Noise Hazards

Roofing jobsites can be loud. Hearing damage may not happen all at once, but repeated exposure to loud noise can cause permanent hearing loss.

Key points

  • Saws, grinders, drills, compressors, generators, and blowers.
  • Heavy equipment, trucks, cranes, forklifts, and hoists.
  • Metal cutting, fastening, demolition, and tear-off work.
  • Noise bouncing off walls, decks, or enclosed areas.
  • Long exposure can be harmful even when the noise feels normal.

Field takeawayIf the work is loud, protect your hearing early.

Section 2 — When to Use Hearing Protection

Hearing protection should be used when noise levels are high or when company or jobsite rules require it. Workers should not wait until their ears hurt.

Key points

  • Use hearing protection around loud tools and equipment.
  • Use hearing protection when you have to raise your voice to talk nearby.
  • Use hearing protection during cutting, grinding, or prolonged equipment operation.
  • Follow posted signs and supervisor instructions.
  • Ask if you are unsure whether hearing protection is required.

Field takeawayHearing damage can happen before you notice it.

Section 3 — Proper Use and Inspection

Earplugs or earmuffs must be worn correctly to work. Poor fit can greatly reduce protection.

Key points

  • Insert earplugs correctly and with clean hands when possible.
  • Make sure earmuffs seal around the ears.
  • Do not remove protection while loud work continues.
  • Replace dirty, damaged, or worn-out protection.
  • Store reusable hearing protection clean and dry.

Field takeawayHearing protection only works when it fits and stays in place.

Quick Knowledge Check

When should a worker put on hearing protection?

  • A. After ears start ringing
  • B. Before loud work begins
  • C. Only at the end of the shift
  • D. Only when standing next to the supervisor

Correct answer: B — Put hearing protection on before loud work begins.

Respiratory Protection and Dust Awareness

Respiratory protection helps protect roofers from breathing hazards such as dust, fumes, fibers, vapors, and spray materials.

Section 1 — Common Breathing Hazards

Roofing tasks can create dust, fumes, vapors, fibers, or mist that workers should not breathe. Some hazards are obvious, while others may not have a strong smell or warning sign.

Key points

  • Dust from cutting, grinding, sweeping, tear-off, or insulation.
  • Fumes or vapors from adhesives, primers, coatings, solvents, or cleaners.
  • Spray materials, mists, or airborne particles.
  • Smoke or fumes from hot work.
  • Unknown materials that should be checked before disturbing.

Field takeawayDo not guess with breathing hazards.

Section 2 — When Respiratory Protection May Be Needed

Respiratory protection may be needed when dust, fumes, vapors, fibers, or spray materials cannot be avoided or controlled. The correct protection depends on the hazard.

Key points

  • Use respiratory protection when required by the task, product, SDS, company, or supervisor.
  • Different hazards require different respirators or filters.
  • A dust mask is not the same as a respirator for every chemical hazard.
  • Better ventilation or work controls may also be needed.
  • Stop and ask before working around unknown breathing hazards.

Field takeawayThe right respirator depends on what is in the air.

Section 3 — Proper Use and Limits

Respirators must fit and be used correctly. The wrong respirator, poor fit, facial hair, damaged straps, or wrong filters can reduce protection.

Key points

  • Use the respirator type assigned for the task.
  • Check straps, seal, filters, and facepiece before use.
  • Facial hair can prevent a proper seal on tight-fitting respirators.
  • Replace filters or cartridges as directed.
  • Leave the area and report problems if breathing becomes difficult or irritation occurs.

Field takeawayA respirator that does not fit correctly may not protect you.

Quick Knowledge Check

What should a worker do before using respiratory protection?

  • A. Guess which mask looks best
  • B. Use any mask found in the truck
  • C. Make sure the respirator is the right type, fits properly, and is required for the hazard
  • D. Hold their breath during dusty work

Correct answer: C — Use the correct respiratory protection for the hazard and make sure it fits properly.

High-Visibility Clothing and Weather Protection

High-visibility clothing and weather protection help roofers stay seen, alert, and protected from heat, cold, sun, rain, and wind.

Section 1 — Visibility and Weather Hazards

Roofers may work around trucks, equipment, loading areas, traffic, crews, and changing weather. Visibility and weather exposure can affect safety and performance.

Key points

  • Workers may be near trucks, forklifts, cranes, deliveries, or equipment.
  • Low light, shadows, rain, dust, or busy jobsites can make workers harder to see.
  • Heat, sun, cold, wind, and rain can affect focus and movement.
  • Wet clothing or heat stress can create safety risks.
  • Weather can change roof conditions quickly.

Field takeawayBeing seen and protected from weather helps keep workers safe.

Section 2 — When High-Visibility Clothing Is Needed

High-visibility clothing helps workers stand out around vehicles, equipment, and active work zones. It may be required by company rules, site rules, or the work environment.

Key points

  • Wear high-vis clothing around moving vehicles or equipment.
  • Wear it in loading zones, staging areas, parking lots, or near traffic.
  • Wear it when visibility is reduced by weather, dust, darkness, or shadows.
  • Keep high-vis clothing visible and not fully covered by other clothing.
  • Replace faded, torn, or dirty high-vis gear when it no longer stands out.

Field takeawayIf people or equipment need to see you, high-vis matters.

Section 3 — Weather Protection Basics

Weather protection is part of jobsite safety. Roofers may need protection from sun, heat, cold, rain, and wind depending on the day and task.

Key points

  • Use sun protection, shade breaks, hydration, and cooling gear when needed.
  • Use warm layers and dry clothing in cold or wet conditions.
  • Use rain gear when appropriate, but watch for reduced traction.
  • Secure loose clothing so it does not catch on tools or materials.
  • Stop and report unsafe weather conditions.

Field takeawayWeather can become a hazard, not just an inconvenience.

Quick Knowledge Check

Why might a roofer need high-visibility clothing?

  • A. To look professional only
  • B. To be easier to see around vehicles, equipment, traffic, or busy jobsites
  • C. To replace a hard hat
  • D. Only when working indoors

Correct answer: B — High-vis clothing helps workers be seen around vehicles, equipment, and active jobsites.

PPE Inspection and Replacement

PPE must be inspected before use because damaged, contaminated, expired, or worn gear may not protect the worker.

Section 1 — Why Inspection Matters

PPE can wear out, break, become contaminated, or lose effectiveness. A worker should not assume PPE is safe just because it is available.

Key points

  • Heat, sunlight, chemicals, water, impact, and daily use can damage PPE.
  • Cracks, tears, worn soles, scratched lenses, and broken straps matter.
  • Contaminated PPE may create a new hazard.
  • Expired or worn-out PPE may not perform as expected.
  • Bad PPE can give false confidence.

Field takeawayInspect PPE before trusting it.

Section 2 — What to Check

Each type of PPE has different inspection points, but the same basic rule applies: look for damage, wear, contamination, missing parts, poor fit, or anything that seems unsafe.

Key points

  • Hard hats: cracks, dents, brittleness, damaged suspension.
  • Eye protection: scratched, cracked, dirty, or loose lenses.
  • Gloves: holes, tears, chemical contamination, worn grip.
  • Footwear: worn soles, damage, poor traction, exposed toe or sole issues.
  • Respirators: damaged straps, dirty facepiece, wrong or expired filters.
  • High-vis clothing: faded, torn, dirty, or no longer visible.

Field takeawaySmall defects can become big problems during the task.

Section 3 — Reporting and Replacement

Damaged PPE should be removed from use and reported. Workers should not keep using gear that may not protect them.

Key points

  • Stop using PPE that is cracked, torn, broken, contaminated, or unsafe.
  • Report damaged PPE to a supervisor.
  • Replace PPE after serious impact or when required by manufacturer guidance.
  • Do not repair PPE in a way the manufacturer does not allow.
  • Keep PPE clean and stored properly when not in use.

Field takeawayIf PPE is bad, stop and replace it.

Quick Knowledge Check

What should a worker do with damaged PPE?

  • A. Keep using it until the end of the week
  • B. Hide it so nobody gets blamed
  • C. Report it and replace it before continuing the task
  • D. Tape it up and assume it is fine

Correct answer: C — Damaged PPE should be reported and replaced.

PPE Field Checklist Before Starting Work

A simple pre-job PPE checklist helps roofers pause, identify hazards, choose the right gear, and start work safely.

Section 1 — Stop Before Starting

Before starting work, take a short pause to think through the task and the hazards. This helps prevent rushing into work with the wrong protection.

Key points

  • What task am I doing?
  • What tools, materials, chemicals, or equipment are involved?
  • What could hit, cut, burn, splash, trip, or expose me?
  • Is anyone working above, below, or near me?
  • Has the roof condition changed?

Field takeawayA short pause can prevent a serious mistake.

Section 2 — Choose and Check PPE

After identifying the hazards, choose PPE that matches the task. Then inspect it before use.

Key points

  • Head protection if objects, overhead hazards, or site rules require it.
  • Eye and face protection for debris, dust, splash, or particles.
  • Gloves matched to cuts, heat, chemicals, or grip needs.
  • Footwear with proper traction and protection.
  • Hearing protection for loud work.
  • Respiratory protection when breathing hazards require it.
  • High-vis or weather protection when needed.

Field takeawayPick PPE based on the hazard, then inspect it before use.

Section 3 — Wear It Correctly and Ask If Unsure

PPE must be worn correctly during the task. If the worker is unsure what PPE is required, the safest move is to stop and ask before continuing.

Key points

  • Put PPE on before starting the task.
  • Keep PPE on while the hazard is present.
  • Adjust PPE so it fits correctly.
  • Replace or report damaged PPE.
  • Ask a supervisor if the required PPE is unclear.
  • Stop work if the hazard changes and PPE no longer matches the task.

Field takeawayDo not guess. Stop and ask before the work starts.

Quick Knowledge Check

What is the best PPE routine before starting roof work?

  • A. Start fast and fix PPE later
  • B. Copy whatever another worker is wearing without thinking
  • C. Identify the task and hazards, choose the right PPE, inspect it, wear it correctly, and ask if unsure
  • D. Only wear PPE after the supervisor complains

Correct answer: C — Check the task, hazards, PPE, condition, fit, and ask if unsure.
